# to the medium house value (Medv).

sns.scatterplot(data = df, x='Medv', y='Crim', ax = ax[0])
sns.scatterplot(data = df, x='Medv', y='Rm', ax = ax[1])

sns.scatterplot(data = df, x='Medv', y='Tax', ax = ax[3])
plt.show()

crim-medv: There is a negative correlation between the crime rate and median house value up to a point. A lower crime rate is associated with a higher median house value up until around $30,000.
Rm-medv: There is a positive correlation between the average number of rooms of a house and the median house value. The higher average number of rooms per unit has is associate with a higher median house value.
Age-medv: There is no clear correlation between the proportion of units built before 1940 and the median house value.
Tax-medv: There is no clear correlation between the property tax rate and the median house value.

Looking at both the plots of the results and at the crosstabs, one can see that k-means is the better method of the two. In the DBSCAN model one can see that a single label dominates the roughly trapezoid-shaped area between sepal length 5.5 and 7.5 whereas the area should have a mix of versicolor and virginica. The crosstab confirms there is plenty of mislabeling with DBSCAN(one label only has 17 instances, versus the even 50-50-50 of the dataset). K-means in turn looks better considering the plot and crosstab.

Why does DBSCAN performs worse? It is not clear from the original plot that a density-based clustering would be better. The intermixing of versicolor and virginica throws off a method dependent on density of points.
